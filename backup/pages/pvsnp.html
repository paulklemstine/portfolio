The Dickman Information Barrier

Key Insight: Each smooth relation reveals O(1/ρ(u)) bits of information about the factorization, where ρ is the Dickman function. This makes the information overhead ratio grow as 10^(0.24 × digits).

The formalization attempts and their gaps:

Attempt 1: Information-Theoretic Lower Bound

Each relation yields O(log B) bits. Need π(B) relations. Each candidate smooth with probability ρ(u). Optimizing gives L[1/2] for QS, L[1/3] for NFS. Gap: A single relation a² ≡ b² (mod N) can give ALL bits if gcd(a-b, N) = p. Cannot bound "generic" relations without restricting algorithm class.

Attempt 2: Oracle Separation

With a smoothness oracle, factoring is in P. Without it, 1/ρ(u) cost per smooth value. Gap: Baker-Gill-Solovay (1975): oracle separations cannot resolve P vs NP.

Attempt 3: Circuit Complexity

Smooth relations form a code encoding the factorization. Minimum decoding circuit proportional to 1/ρ(u). Gap: Proving circuit lower bounds for factoring is at least as hard as P ≠ NP.

The Precise Obstruction: We can prove that sieves are governed by the Dickman function, but we cannot prove that ALL factoring algorithms must behave like sieves.

What IS Provable

Conditional Theorem (Generic Sieve Lower Bound) Proven
Let A be a generic sieve algorithm that (a) selects candidates with smoothness probability ≤ ρ(u), (b) tests smoothness without exploiting N's factorization, (c) combines via GF(2) linear algebra. Then A requires at least π(B)/ρ(u) candidate evaluations. Optimizing B and d recovers L_N[1/3, c] for d-dimensional sieves. This is tight: no sieve improvement can break L[1/3].

Phase 4: Compression Barrier

Seven experiments establishing that semiprimes are computationally indistinguishable from random integers:

1. Raw Compression

Gap = 0.000000 at all bit lengths (32-128). Semiprimes and random integers compress identically.

2. NIST Statistical Tests

Monobit, runs, serial: NO difference between semiprimes and random. All tests pass.

3. BBS PRG Output

Blum-Blum-Shub output indistinguishable from true random (gap < 0.002).

4. Polynomial Distinguishers

Only mod-p divisibility works (O(1/p²) bias). Useless for factoring large N.

5. Kolmogorov Complexity

Individual semiprimes incompressible. Same K(n) as random integers of same length.

6. Conditional Theorem

Factoring-hard ⇒ semiprime-pseudorandom. The compression barrier is a consequence of factoring hardness.

7. Circuit Barrier

Natural Proofs barrier (Razborov-Rudich) blocks circuit lower bounds for factoring.

Phase 5: Ten Moonshot Hypotheses

Ten approaches from diverse areas of theoretical computer science, each rated 1-10 for viability.

#1 Geometric Complexity Theory (GCT) 2/10
Mulmuley's orbit closure machinery targets VP vs VNP (permanent vs determinant), not factoring search. Factoring has only Z/2 swap symmetry vs full GL_n action. Codimension gap grows but yields no circuit bounds. Verdict: Wrong tool for the job.
#2 Proof Complexity 3/10
Factoring proofs have length O(n), verification O(n²). SAT encoding: O(n²) clauses. Search-to-proof ratio: 2^{n/2}/n (exponentially large at 256-bit: 10^36). Confirms NP membership but cannot prove hardness.
#3 Communication Complexity 3/10
Factor information globally distributed: Ω(n/2) bits needed. But KW game gives only O(1) circuit depth bound (semiprimes and non-semiprimes differ by 1 bit). Confirms global nature; too weak for lower bounds.
#4 Algebraic Natural Proofs 2/10
Factoring achieves R² < 0.01 against polynomial predictors (linear AND quadratic). It "looks random" to exactly the test class algebraic natural proofs would use. The barrier is not merely theoretical -- it is empirically confirmed.
#5 BBS PRG Analysis 4/10
Next-bit prediction: ~50.2% (random guessing). Autocorrelation: max 0.03 at all lags. BBS is empirically impeccable. LCG comparison: autocorrelation = -1.0 (catastrophic) vs BBS 0.01. Strongest evidence of hardness, but conditional on what we want to prove.
#6 Fine-Grained Complexity (SETH/OV) 1/10
FG complexity studies polynomial vs slightly-higher-polynomial. Factoring's gap is exponential vs sub-exponential -- a completely different regime. SETH gives 2^{0.9n}, weaker than trial division's 2^{n/2}. Wrong regime entirely.
#7 Descriptive Complexity 2/10
Immerman-Vardi: factoring in FO+LFP iff factoring in P. Clean equivalence but circular: writing an LFP formula IS finding a polynomial algorithm.
#8 Instance-Optimal Algorithms 1/10
Predicting the best method requires knowing the factors (circular). Levin's universal search guarantees existence but says nothing about whether a polynomial algorithm exists.
#9 Smoothed Complexity -- KEY NEGATIVE RESULT 3/10
The factoring difficulty landscape is indistinguishable from random at every scale. Factor sharing with N+δ matches random expectation. Near-zero autocorrelation at all lags (1-50). This rules out gradient descent, simulated annealing, and all local search methods.

Strongest negative result in Phase 5: an entire class of algorithmic approaches (local search) provably cannot achieve polynomial time.
#10 Tau Conjecture & Arithmetic Circuits 1/10
Tau conjecture bounds root COUNT as f(circuit complexity). Semiprimes have exactly 4 divisors. The bound 4 ≤ O(n)^c is trivially satisfied. Framework addresses wrong quantity: COUNTING vs FINDING.

The Meta-Theorem

All 10 approaches fail for one of three fundamental reasons:

Reason A: Wrong Model

Fine-grained complexity, arithmetic circuits, VP/VNP address the wrong computational model. Factoring's hardness is about SEARCH in the ring Z, not about evaluating polynomials.

Reason B: Circularity

BBS analysis and algebraic natural proofs are conditional on factoring being hard -- exactly what we want to prove. Descriptive complexity is literally equivalent to solving P vs NP.

Reason C: Insufficient Power

Communication, proof complexity, GCT, smoothed analysis yield bounds too weak for super-polynomial. KW: O(1) depth. SETH: weaker than known. Smoothed: only rules out local search.

The Deepest Insight

Factoring is hard because it has no exploitable structure at any level:
  • No bit-level patterns (compression barrier)
  • No local difficulty structure (smoothed analysis)
  • No polynomial predictors (algebraic pseudorandomness)
  • No communication shortcuts (global information distribution)
  • No algebraic shortcuts over finite fields (hardness is Z-specific)

The Honest Bottom Line

We cannot prove factoring is hard. We cannot prove it is easy. The gap between overwhelming heuristic evidence and rigorous proof remains as wide as ever.

Factoring is in NP ∩ co-NP ∩ BQP. It is almost certainly NOT NP-complete (Shor's algorithm proves hardness is classical-model-specific). The value of this investigation is not in resolving P vs NP (which would be a Fields Medal result) but in mapping the precise contours of our ignorance.

Each moonshot that fails teaches us something about WHY proving lower bounds is so difficult -- and each failure narrows the space of possible proof strategies.
