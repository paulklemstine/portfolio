QI1: Quadratic Irrational Convergence

Proven High Significance

Formal Statement

Repeating Berggren paths converge to specific quadratic irrational values determined by the dominant eigenvector of the corresponding matrix product:

$B^{2k}$ → $1 + \sqrt{2} \approx 2.4142...$
$(B^2 \cdot B^1)^k$ → $\phi + 1 = (3 + \sqrt{5})/2 \approx 2.618...$
$A^k \to 1$ (degenerate)

In general, for any periodic path word W, the ratio $c_k/a_k$ converges to a quadratic irrational $\alpha(W) = (p + q\sqrt{d})/r$ that is the ratio of eigenvector components of the matrix product M(W).

Intuition (Plain English)

If you always take the same branch in the Berggren tree (say, always B), the triples you generate settle into a pattern. The ratio c/a (hypotenuse to odd leg) approaches a fixed irrational number. For pure B-paths, that number is $1 + \sqrt{2}$.

This is the matrix equivalent of "repeated squaring converges to an eigenvector." When you multiply the same matrix M by itself many times, the result is dominated by M's largest eigenvalue and corresponding eigenvector. The eigenvector components give the limiting ratios.

The beautiful part: every periodic path gives a different quadratic irrational. The golden ratio $\phi$ appears when you alternate B and A. This means the Berggren tree is a dictionary of quadratic irrationals, each one indexed by a periodic path word.

Proof Sketch

Step 1: For a periodic word W of length L, compute $M = M(w_L) \cdot \ldots \cdot M(w_1)$. This is a 3x3 integer matrix with spectral radius $\rho > 1$.

Step 2: By the Perron-Frobenius theorem (extended to positive matrices), $M^k v \to \rho^k (v \cdot u) w$, where w is the dominant eigenvector. The ratio $c_k/a_k = w_3/w_1$.

Step 3: The characteristic polynomial of M is cubic with integer coefficients. The dominant eigenvalue $\rho$ is a root of this cubic. The eigenvector components involve $\sqrt{d}$ where d is the discriminant of the minimal polynomial of $\rho$. Hence c/a converges to a quadratic irrational.

Step 4 -- Example: $B^2$ has eigenvalues $1, 3+2\sqrt{2}, 3-2\sqrt{2}$. Dominant eigenvector proportional to $(1, 2+\sqrt{2}, 1+\sqrt{2})$, so $c/a \to (1+\sqrt{2})/1 = 1+\sqrt{2}$.

Significance

Links the Berggren tree to algebraic number theory. Each periodic path encodes a quadratic field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$, and the convergence rate is governed by the eigenvalue gap of the path matrix.

For CFRAC: this is the tree-theoretic explanation of why periodic continued fractions give quadratic irrationals (Lagrange's theorem). The CFRAC-Tree equivalence theorem makes this precise.

For factoring: knowing that certain paths converge to specific irrationals tells us which paths are likely to produce values near $\sqrt{N}$, which is exactly where smooth values concentrate.
