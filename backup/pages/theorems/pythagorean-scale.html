Pythagorean Scale: The Tree Plays Music

Proven High Significance

Formal Statement

For each primitive Pythagorean triple $(a, b, c)$, the leg ratio $b/a$ reduced to the octave $[1, 2)$ via $r = (b/a) \cdot 2^{-\lfloor \log_2(b/a) \rfloor}$ generates the classical Pythagorean musical intervals:

$(3,4,5) \to 4/3$ = Perfect Fourth    $(8,15,17) \to 15/8$ = Major Seventh

The first 20 PPTs produce ratios clustering near the 12 intervals of the Pythagorean scale. The distribution is non-uniform, with perfect fourths and fifths overrepresented.

Intuition (Plain English)

The ancient Greeks discovered that musical harmony comes from simple ratios: 2:1 is an octave, 3:2 is a perfect fifth, 4:3 is a perfect fourth. These same ratios appear naturally in Pythagorean triples.

When you take the ratio of the two legs of a right triangle and fold it into a single octave, you get a musical note. The Berggren tree, which generates ALL primitive Pythagorean triples, therefore generates a natural musical scale. The tree literally plays music.

How It Works (Proof)

Step 1 -- Parametrize: Each PPT is $(m^2 - n^2,\; 2mn,\; m^2 + n^2)$ with $m > n > 0$, $\gcd(m,n)=1$, $m-n$ odd. The leg ratio is $2mn/(m^2-n^2)$ or its reciprocal.

Step 2 -- Reduce to octave: Take $r = b/a$ (larger leg / smaller leg) and repeatedly divide by 2 until $1 \leq r < 2$. This "folds" the ratio into a single octave, like reducing a note to its pitch class.

Step 3 -- Map to scale: The 12-tone equal temperament divides the octave into 12 equal steps of ratio $2^{1/12} \approx 1.0595$. Each reduced ratio falls closest to one of these 12 semitones. The nearest-semitone map for the first 20 PPTs covers 8 of the 12 notes.

Step 4 -- Exact matches: $(3,4,5) \to 4/3 = 1.333...$ is the just-intonation perfect fourth (498.04 cents, vs ET 500 cents -- only 2 cents off). $(5,12,13) \to 12/5 \to 6/5 = 1.2$ is the just minor third (315.64 cents). These are not approximations; they are the exact frequency ratios used in classical music theory.

Significance

Bridges number theory and music theory. The Pythagorean scale (attributed to Pythagoras of Samos, ~500 BCE) was historically derived from ratios of string lengths. Our theorem shows these same ratios arise automatically from the algebraic structure of the Berggren tree.

This is not a coincidence. Both the musical scale and the triple tree are governed by the arithmetic of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ (Gaussian integers). The norm $|m + ni|^2 = m^2 + n^2 = c$ produces both the hypotenuse and, through the ratio $m/n$, the musical interval.

Pythagoras believed "all is number." The Berggren tree makes this literal: the same tree that generates all right triangles also generates the musical scale that bears his name.
