Twin Hypotenuse Primes are Impossible

Proven High Significance
Theorem. There are no twin primes $(p, p+2)$ where both $p$ and $p+2$ are hypotenuses of primitive Pythagorean triples. If $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ is a prime hypotenuse, then $p + 2 \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, which can never be a sum of two squares.

Intuition

A prime can be a PPT hypotenuse only if it equals 1 mod 4 (Fermat's theorem on sums of two squares). But if $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, then $p + 2 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$. And primes that are 3 mod 4 can never be written as $a^2 + b^2$. So the mod-4 structure of primes creates an impenetrable barrier: hypotenuse primes are always separated by at least 4.

Proof

Step 1: By Fermat's theorem on sums of two squares, a prime $p$ is expressible as $p = a^2 + b^2$ if and only if $p = 2$ or $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$.

Step 2: A prime hypotenuse of a PPT must satisfy $c = m^2 + n^2$ with $\gcd(m,n) = 1$ and $m \not\equiv n \pmod{2}$. Since $c$ is an odd prime, $c \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$.

Step 3: If $c \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, then $c + 2 \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$. A prime $q \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ cannot be written as a sum of two squares, hence cannot be a PPT hypotenuse.

Step 4: Therefore no twin prime pair $(p, p+2)$ can have both members as PPT hypotenuses. QED.

Significance

This elegant impossibility result shows that the mod-4 arithmetic constraint on hypotenuses creates a fundamental gap structure. While the twin prime conjecture remains open for general primes, for hypotenuse primes the question is trivially resolved: twin hypotenuse primes simply cannot exist.

The minimum gap between consecutive hypotenuse primes is 4 (e.g., 5 and 13 have gap 8; 13 and 17 have gap 4). This connects to Dirichlet's theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions.
