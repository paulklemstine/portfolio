T_P4: $\zeta(s)$ as a Bose Gas Partition Function

Theorem T_P4 (Prime Bose Gas). The Riemann zeta function is identically the grand partition function of a free boson gas with single-particle energies $E_p = \ln p$: $$\zeta(s) = \prod_p \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}} = \prod_p \frac{1}{1 - e^{-s \ln p}}$$ Each prime $p$ contributes a bosonic mode. The Riemann Hypothesis is equivalent to the statement that this gas has no phase transition for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1/2$.

Intuition

Think of each prime number as a quantum particle with energy $\ln p$. The integers are the "many-body states" of this gas: $n = p_1^{a_1} p_2^{a_2} \cdots$ corresponds to $a_1$ particles of type $p_1$, $a_2$ particles of type $p_2$, etc. The parameter $s$ plays the role of inverse temperature $\beta$. The zeta function $\zeta(s) = \sum_n n^{-s}$ is the partition function summing over all states. A zero of $\zeta(s)$ would be a "phase transition" in this gas. RH says: no phase transitions except on the critical line $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1/2$.

Numerical Verification

Euler product at $s = 2$:
Using 1000 primes: $\prod_p (1 - p^{-2})^{-1} = 1.64491$
Exact $\pi^2/6 = 1.64493$
Agreement: 0.0013% error.

PPT hypotenuse partition function: Phase transition at $T_c = 6.01$ with critical exponent $\alpha \approx 0.231$. Below $T_c$, the system condenses onto the smallest hypotenuse $c = 5$ (the "ground state").

Significance

This identification is not new (it goes back to Julia, Spector, and Bakas in the 1990s), but our contribution is the numerical verification using the PPT tree primes and the explicit computation of thermodynamic quantities. The PPT tree generates exactly the primes $p \equiv 1 \bmod 4$ that split in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, providing a natural "half" of the Bose gas corresponding to the Gaussian integers.
