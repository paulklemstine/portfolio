QR Reciprocity on PPTs: Always +1

Proven High Significance
Theorem. For all primitive Pythagorean triples $(a, b, c)$ with $\gcd(a,c) = 1$, the Jacobi symbol product satisfies $$\left(\frac{a}{c}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{c}{a}\right) = +1 \text{ always.}$$

Intuition

Quadratic reciprocity is one of the deepest results in number theory -- Gauss called it the "golden theorem." It relates whether $a$ is a square mod $c$ to whether $c$ is a square mod $a$. The reciprocity formula involves $(-1)^{(a-1)(c-1)/4}$.

For PPTs, the hypotenuse $c$ always satisfies $c \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ (since $c = m^2 + n^2$ with opposite parities). This makes $(c-1)/4$ an integer, and the exponent $(a-1)(c-1)/4$ is always even. So the reciprocity sign is always $+1$.

Proof

Step 1: By quadratic reciprocity (Gauss), for odd coprime $a, c$: $\left(\frac{a}{c}\right)\left(\frac{c}{a}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{a-1}{2}\cdot\frac{c-1}{2}}$.

Step 2: Every PPT hypotenuse satisfies $c = m^2 + n^2$ where $m > n$, $\gcd(m,n) = 1$, $m \not\equiv n \pmod{2}$. One of $m, n$ is even, so $m^2 + n^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, giving $(c-1)/2 \equiv 0 \pmod{2}$.

Step 3: Since $(c-1)/2$ is even, the exponent $\frac{a-1}{2} \cdot \frac{c-1}{2}$ is always even regardless of $a$.

Step 4: Therefore $(-1)^{\text{even}} = +1$. QED.

Significance

This is a universal structural identity connecting the Berggren tree to quadratic reciprocity. It holds for ALL primitive Pythagorean triples, not just those with prime hypotenuse. The $c \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ constraint is the key: it forces the reciprocity sign to always be positive, revealing that the tree's arithmetic is governed by this mod-4 structure.
