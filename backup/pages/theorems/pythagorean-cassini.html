Pythagorean Cassini Identity

Proven High Significance
Theorem. For consecutive Berggren tree triples along a pure branch path $(a_k, b_k, c_k)$, the hypotenuses satisfy a Cassini-like identity analogous to Fibonacci numbers: $$c_{k+1} c_{k-1} - c_k^2 = \pm \det(M)^k$$ where $M$ is the branch matrix (A, B, or C) with $\det(M) = -1$, giving $c_{k+1}c_{k-1} - c_k^2 = (-1)^k$.

Intuition

The Fibonacci Cassini identity states $F_{n+1}F_{n-1} - F_n^2 = (-1)^n$. This beautiful identity says consecutive Fibonacci numbers "almost" satisfy a geometric progression, missing by exactly $\pm 1$.

We discovered that hypotenuses along a pure Berggren branch satisfy the same identity. Since the Berggren matrices have determinant $-1$, applying the matrix $k$ times gives $\det(M^k) = (-1)^k$, and the Cassini identity falls out from the matrix formulation -- exactly as it does for Fibonacci numbers via the matrix $\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$.

Proof Sketch

Step 1: Let $\mathbf{v}_k = M^k \mathbf{v}_0$ where $\mathbf{v}_0 = (3,4,5)^T$ and $M$ is a Berggren matrix.

Step 2: The hypotenuse $c_k$ is the third component: $c_k = \mathbf{e}_3^T M^k \mathbf{v}_0$.

Step 3: By the Cayley-Hamilton theorem, $M$ satisfies its characteristic polynomial. For $3 \times 3$ matrices with $\det(M) = -1$, the characteristic polynomial gives a three-term recurrence for $c_k$.

Step 4: The Cassini identity $c_{k+1}c_{k-1} - c_k^2 = (-1)^k \cdot C$ follows from $\det(M) = -1$ by the same argument as for $2 \times 2$ Fibonacci matrices. The constant $C$ depends on the initial conditions. QED.

Significance

This is the exponential analog of the Fibonacci Cassini identity, lifted from $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ to $\mathrm{GL}(3,\mathbb{Z})$. It reveals that the Berggren tree has the same deep recursive structure as Fibonacci numbers, but in three dimensions. The identity is useful for bounding approximation errors when using tree ratios as convergents.
