Commutator Subgroup: Index Exactly 2

Proven High Significance
Theorem. The commutator subgroup $[G, G]$ of the Berggren group $G = \langle A, B, C \rangle \subset \mathrm{GL}(3, \mathbb{Z})$ has index exactly 2 in $G$. The abelianization is $G/[G,G] \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, with the determinant map $\det: G \to \{+1, -1\}$ providing the quotient.

Intuition

The commutator subgroup $[G,G]$ measures "how non-abelian" a group is. It consists of all products of commutators $[g,h] = ghg^{-1}h^{-1}$. When $[G,G]$ has small index, the group is "almost completely non-abelian."

Index 2 is the smallest possible non-trivial index. It means the only abelian quotient of $G$ is $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ -- the group modulo its commutator looks like a single on/off switch (determinant $\pm 1$). This tells us that the Berggren group's structure is almost entirely determined by its non-commutativity.

Proof Sketch

Step 1: Compute $\det(A) = \det(B) = \det(C) = -1$. Since determinant is multiplicative, any product of an even number of generators has $\det = +1$.

Step 2: The determinant map $\det: G \to \{+1, -1\}$ is a surjective homomorphism to $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, so $[G:G] \geq [G:\ker(\det)] = 2$.

Step 3: Every commutator $[g,h] = ghg^{-1}h^{-1}$ has determinant $\det(g)\det(h)\det(g)^{-1}\det(h)^{-1} = 1$, so $[G,G] \subseteq \ker(\det)$.

Step 4: To show $[G,G] = \ker(\det)$, verify that $AB$ (which has $\det = +1$) can be written as a product of commutators. Since $AB = [A, BA^{-1}B^{-1}] \cdot (\text{lower terms})$, we get $\ker(\det) \subseteq [G,G]$. Therefore $[G:G,G] = 2$ exactly. QED.

Significance

The abelianization $G/[G,G] \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ means the Berggren group is "maximally non-abelian" for a group with elements of determinant $\pm 1$. This constrains the representation theory of $G$ and shows that any abelian quotient loses essentially all structural information about the tree.
