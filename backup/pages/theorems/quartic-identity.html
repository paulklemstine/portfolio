NI1: Quartic Identity $a^4 + b^4 - c^4 = -2a^2 b^2$

Proven High Significance

Formal Statement

For every primitive Pythagorean triple $(a, b, c)$ with $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$:

$a^4 + b^4 - c^4 = -2a^2 b^2$

This is an exact algebraic identity, holding for ALL Pythagorean triples (primitive or not).

Intuition (Plain English)

Fermat proved that $a^4 + b^4 = c^4$ has no solutions in positive integers (the $n=4$ case of Fermat's Last Theorem). But what IS $a^4 + b^4 - c^4$ for Pythagorean triples?

The answer is beautifully simple: it equals $-2a^2 b^2$. This means the "deficit" from the quartic Fermat equation is exactly twice the square of the triangle's area (since area $= ab/2$, we get $-2a^2b^2 = -8 \cdot \text{area}^2$).

In other words: Pythagorean triples are the points where Fermat's quartic equation has a deficit of exactly $2a^2b^2$. No more, no less.

Proof

Given: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.

Square both sides:

$(a^2 + b^2)^2 = c^4$

$a^4 + 2a^2 b^2 + b^4 = c^4$

Rearrange:

$a^4 + b^4 - c^4 = -2a^2 b^2$

QED. The proof is a single algebraic manipulation.

Significance

Connections:

  • Fermat's Last Theorem: Quantifies the exact distance from $a^4 + b^4 = c^4$ (which has no solutions) to Pythagorean triples (which have infinitely many solutions at $n=2$)
  • Area connection: Since $-2a^2b^2 = -8(\text{area})^2$, the quartic deficit encodes the triangle's area
  • Norm identity: In $\mathbb{Z}[i]$: $|z|^4 = (\text{Re}(z^2))^2 + (\text{Im}(z^2))^2$ when $z = a + bi$ and $|z|^2 = c$
  • Higher powers: Generalizes to $\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k}(-1)^{n-k} a^{2k} b^{2(n-k)} = 0$ for all even $n$, providing a family of identities
