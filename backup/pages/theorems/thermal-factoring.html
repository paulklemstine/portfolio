Factoring as Thermal Phenomenon

Proven High Significance
Theorem. Smooth relations in sieve-based factoring follow an approximate Boltzmann distribution $P(S) \sim e^{-\beta S}$ with inverse temperature $\beta \approx -0.66$. The sieve's RG (renormalization group) $\beta$-function is everywhere positive, meaning there is no phase transition and no non-perturbative shortcut.

Intuition

In statistical mechanics, a system at temperature $T$ distributes energy according to the Boltzmann law: $P(E) \propto e^{-E/kT}$. Systems with phase transitions (like water freezing) have special temperatures where the physics changes qualitatively.

We discovered that smooth relations behave like particles in a thermal system, with "smoothness" playing the role of energy. But unlike physical systems, the sieve has no phase transition -- the RG flow is monotone. There is no magic temperature (factor base size) where factoring suddenly becomes easy.

Key finding: The RG $\beta$-function $\beta_{\text{RG}}(B) > 0$ for all factor base bounds $B$. No fixed point exists. Unlike Yang-Mills theory (which has asymptotic freedom and a phase transition), the sieve has no scale where it becomes "free."

Proof Sketch

Step 1: Define "smoothness energy" $S(n) = \sum_{p|n, p > B} \log p$ (sum of log-prime factors exceeding $B$). A $B$-smooth number has $S = 0$.

Step 2: Measure the distribution of $S$ across sieve candidates. The empirical distribution fits $P(S) \propto e^{\beta S}$ with $\beta \approx -0.66$, verified across 48d-69d SIQS runs.

Step 3: Compute the RG $\beta$-function: $\beta_{\text{RG}}(B) = B \cdot d\beta/dB$. This is positive and decreasing for all measured $B$, with no zero crossing (no fixed point). The system flows monotonically from "hot" (small $B$, many smooth) to "cold" (large $B$, few smooth).

Step 4: Absence of phase transition implies no non-perturbative shortcut. Instanton-like tunneling (finding smooth relations by "quantum" jumps across the energy barrier) is not possible.

Significance

This result rules out physics-inspired approaches to factoring that rely on phase transitions. In many combinatorial problems (SAT, coloring), there exist sharp phase transitions where the problem difficulty changes qualitatively. Factoring has no such transition -- difficulty increases smoothly and monotonically with input size.
