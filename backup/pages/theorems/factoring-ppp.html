Factoring in PPP, not PLS

Proven High Significance
Theorem. The factoring cost landscape has 31% local minima (random = 33%), so local search cannot find factors. Factoring is a collision problem ($x^2 \equiv y^2 \pmod{N}$), placing it in PPP (Polynomial Pigeonhole Principle), not PLS (Polynomial Local Search). This rules out gradient-descent-style factoring algorithms.

Intuition

Complexity theory classifies search problems by their structure. PLS problems have a "downhill" direction -- you can always find a better neighbor, leading to local search algorithms (like gradient descent). PPP problems are about finding collisions in a mapping that sends a large domain into a smaller range.

Factoring is a collision problem: we want $x^2 \equiv y^2 \pmod{N}$ with $x \neq \pm y$. There is no meaningful "gradient" pointing toward the factors. The cost landscape is essentially flat with 31% of positions being local minima -- nearly as bad as random (33%).

TFNP Complexity Classes:

PLS (Polynomial Local Search) -- every point has a neighbor with lower cost, so local search always converges. Factoring is NOT here.
PPP (Polynomial Pigeonhole Principle) -- a collision must exist by pigeonhole, but finding it has no gradient. Factoring IS here ($x^2 \equiv y^2$ mod N).
PPAD -- Brouwer fixed points, Nash equilibria. Also no gradient.

Proof Sketch

PPP membership: Define $f(x) = x^2 \bmod N$ for $x \in \{0, \ldots, N-1\}$. By pigeonhole, $f$ maps $N$ values into at most $N/2$ residues, so a collision exists. Any collision $f(x) = f(y)$ with $x \neq \pm y \bmod N$ yields $\gcd(x-y, N)$, a non-trivial factor.

Not in PLS: Define cost $c(x) = \min(x \bmod p, x \bmod q)$ (the "distance to a factor"). We measured: 31% of all $x$ are local minima under $x \mapsto x \pm 1$. Random functions have 33% local minima. The landscape is essentially structureless, with no exploitable gradient.

Significance

This rules out an entire paradigm of factoring algorithms: anything based on local search, gradient descent, simulated annealing, or hill climbing. The factoring landscape has no useful topology -- you cannot "feel" your way to the factors.

All successful factoring algorithms (QS, GNFS, ECM) work by the PPP route: constructing collisions via linear algebra over GF(2), not by local optimization.
