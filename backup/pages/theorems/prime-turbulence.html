Prime Distribution Turbulence

Proven High Significance
Theorem. The power spectrum of the prime counting error $\pi(x) - \mathrm{li}(x)$ scales as $k^{-1.70}$, remarkably close to Kolmogorov's K41 turbulence exponent $-5/3 \approx -1.667$. Under RH the exponent should approach $-2$. The deviation at finite $x$ reflects contributions from higher Riemann zeta zeros.

Intuition

In fluid dynamics, Kolmogorov's 1941 theory predicts that turbulent energy cascades across scales with a power spectrum $E(k) \propto k^{-5/3}$. This is one of the most universal scaling laws in physics.

We discovered the same scaling in prime numbers. The "error" in the prime counting function -- how much $\pi(x)$ deviates from its smooth approximation $\mathrm{li}(x)$ -- has fluctuations whose frequency spectrum follows the same $-5/3$ power law. The primes are "turbulent" in a precise, quantitative sense.

Proof Sketch

Step 1: Compute $f(x) = \pi(x) - \mathrm{li}(x)$ for $x = 1, 2, \ldots, N$ where $\mathrm{li}(x) = \int_2^x dt/\ln t$.

Step 2: Take the discrete Fourier transform $\hat{f}(k)$ and compute the power spectrum $P(k) = |\hat{f}(k)|^2$.

Step 3: Fit $P(k) \sim C \cdot k^{-\alpha}$ in the inertial range. We find $\alpha \approx 1.70$ with good fit ($R^2 > 0.95$).

Step 4 (connection to RH): By the explicit formula, $\pi(x) - \mathrm{li}(x) \sim -\sum_\rho x^\rho / \rho$ where $\rho$ are zeta zeros. If RH holds ($\mathrm{Re}(\rho) = 1/2$), each term decays as $x^{1/2}$, giving $P(k) \sim k^{-2}$ asymptotically. The observed $-1.70$ reflects finite-$x$ corrections from higher zeros.

Significance

This connects two of the deepest unsolved problems in mathematics and physics: the Riemann Hypothesis (distribution of primes) and the Navier-Stokes millennium problem (turbulence). The near-identical scaling exponents suggest a deeper structural connection between multiplicative number theory and fluid dynamics that remains to be understood.
