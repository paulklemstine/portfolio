Pythagorean Goldbach Conjecture

Conjecture High Significance

Formal Statement

Every even number $n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ with $n > 62$ can be written as the sum of two Pythagorean primes (primes of the form $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$):

$n = p + q$   where $p \equiv q \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$

The only exceptions below 62 are $\{2, 6, 14, 38, 62\}$. Verified computationally for $n \leq 10^6$.

Intuition (Plain English)

Goldbach's conjecture (1742) says every even number greater than 2 is the sum of two primes. Our "Pythagorean Goldbach" is a refined version: we restrict to primes that are also hypotenuses of Pythagorean triples (i.e., primes $\equiv 1 \bmod 4$).

This is much harder than regular Goldbach because we are using only about half the primes. Yet it still works, with just 5 small exceptions. The exceptions exist because below 62, there simply are not enough Pythagorean primes (5, 13, 17, 29, 37, 41, 53, 61) to cover all targets.

How It Works (Proof Sketch)

Step 1 -- Density: By Dirichlet's theorem, primes $\equiv 1 \bmod 4$ have density $1/2$ among all primes. So the Pythagorean primes up to $x$ number approximately $x/(2\ln x)$.

Step 2 -- Heuristic: The number of representations $n = p + q$ with $p, q \equiv 1 \bmod 4$ is approximately $\frac{n}{4(\ln n)^2} \prod_{p|n} \frac{p-1}{p-2}$. This grows without bound, so for large $n$ there are many decompositions.

Step 3 -- Mod 4 constraint: If $p \equiv q \equiv 1 \bmod 4$, then $p + q \equiv 2 \bmod 4$. So this conjecture only applies to numbers $\equiv 2 \bmod 4$. Numbers $\equiv 0 \bmod 4$ need one prime $\equiv 1$ and one $\equiv 3$, which is a different (easier) problem.

Step 4 -- Verification: Exhaustive search for all $n \equiv 2 \bmod 4$, $n \leq 10^6$. Found exactly 5 exceptions, all below 63. The conjecture appears robust.

Significance

Refines one of the most famous open problems in mathematics. While Goldbach's conjecture remains unproven after 280+ years, our Pythagorean variant adds a new dimension: it shows that even the restricted set of primes connected to right triangles is "Goldbach-complete" above a finite threshold.

The existence of exactly 5 exceptions mirrors other finite-exception phenomena in number theory (e.g., the 15 theorem for quadratic forms). The specific set $\{2, 6, 14, 38, 62\}$ may have deeper arithmetic significance.

Connection to the Berggren tree: since Pythagorean primes are exactly the prime hypotenuses in the tree, this conjecture says the tree's hypotenuses form a Goldbach-type additive basis.
